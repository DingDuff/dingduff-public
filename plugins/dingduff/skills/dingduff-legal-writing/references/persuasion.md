# Persuasion

The advocacy layer — applied to persuasive documents only (briefs, motions, demand letters); **predictive memos stay objective**. Persuasion is layered *onto* a correct, well-organized analysis, never instead of one. A vivid brief on a weak argument loses; persuasion gives a winning argument its full force.

## Credibility and candor are the foundation

Ethos — the reader's trust — is the precondition for every other move, and it is fragile:
- **Never misstate the law or the facts.** One caught overstatement taints the whole document.
- **Disclose adverse controlling authority** and confront damaging facts; the advocate who concedes the fair point is believed on the contested one.
- **No overstatement, no ad hominem.** "Clearly/obviously" and attacks on opposing counsel signal weakness.
- Favorable framing and emphasis are legitimate; **distortion is not.** The line: would the characterization survive the reader checking it against the record?

## The three appeals

- **Logos** — sound legal reasoning (the substance, from `dingduff-legal-analysis`). The heart; the other two amplify it.
- **Ethos** — credibility: accuracy, fairness, candor, command of the record, professional tone. Earned throughout, lost in an instant.
- **Pathos** — the values and (restrained) emotion that make your result feel *just*. In legal writing, carried mostly by the facts and theme, not adjectives.

## The moves

1. **Theme** — state the case's just, common-sense through-line in a sentence (from the theory of the case). Sound it in the introduction, the facts, each argument section, and the conclusion.
2. **Tell the story (facts)** — render the facts as a coherent narrative (sympathetic protagonist, conflict, stakes, the resolution the law should supply): concrete detail for favorable facts; unfavorable facts handled fairly but without spotlight (placement and proportion, never omission); accurate throughout.
3. **Frame the rule and issue** — state them in the light most favorable to your position *that the authority honestly supports* (emphasize the elements you meet; the standard read your way; the issue posed so its answer favors you).
4. **Emphasis** — use the positions of emphasis (openings and closings of the document, sections, paragraphs, and sentences — the end is the stress position). Put your strongest material and best facts there; place unavoidable weak material briefly in the middle. Give winning points more airtime than losing ones.
5. **Identification** — lead the decision-maker to identify with your client and position: align the result with the court's role and values (restraint, fidelity to text, fairness, workable rules); show ruling your way is the principled, ordinary thing to do; present your client as the reasonable party.
6. **Equity and policy, welded to the law** — show the result is fair (equity) and produces good consequences (policy), always tied to the legal hook ("the statute should be read this way *because* the alternative is unjust/unworkable"). Free-floating fairness pleas read as conceding the law is against you.

## Ethos checklist (don't break credibility)

No misstatement of law or fact; adverse controlling authority disclosed and addressed; damaging facts confronted, not hidden; no "clearly/obviously" doing an argument's work; no attacks on opposing counsel or the lower court's good faith; concessions made where fair.

## Going further (high-stakes advocacy)

Develop a full **theory of the case** (the stock story the facts evoke; character framing for every actor; the theme as a repeated motif seeded into headings and topic sentences). **Tailor to the audience** — trial judge (busy, fact-and-equity focused) vs. appellate panel (rule and consequences) vs. the particular bench (known values). Work the **style levers** sparingly (controlled repetition, parallelism, an apt fresh metaphor; sentence rhythm). **Anticipate and defuse** the opponent's theme and best equitable points, pre-empting them where they do least damage.
