# Document Components

The reusable parts shared across memos and briefs: question presented, statement of facts, point headings, and standard of review.

---

## Question Presented (+ Brief Answer)

The issue statement at the top of a memo or brief. A good one is **one question** folding in the **governing standard**, the **legally significant facts**, and the **precise legal question**, readable in one pass.

- **One question per issue** (multi-issue → numbered). Include the legally significant facts (a question divorced from facts is useless). Predictive: neutral, no assumed answer. Persuasive: framed to suggest the answer but accurate.
- **Predictive form — "under / does / when":** *Under [governing law], does [the precise question], when [the significant facts]?* (Variations: under-is-when, under-can-when.) Example: *Under [state] premises-liability law, does a store owe a duty to warn of a spill when an employee saw it twenty minutes before the customer slipped but posted no warning?*
- **Brief Answer (memos):** lead with the bottom line (*Yes / No / Probably…*), then one short paragraph — rule + determinative reason, no citations. Must answer the question and match the Discussion.
- **Persuasive form (briefs):** a "Whether…" clause or a positive statement form that incorporates favorable framing and helpful facts; lead toward your answer through *selection and emphasis*, never misstatement.

Faults: no facts; conclusory (assumes the contested point); multi-part run-on; over-slanted (persuasive); a brief answer that dodges or contradicts the Discussion.

## Statement of Facts

Where most documents are won or lost — the reader forms an impression here. Build it from the classified facts in `dingduff-legal-analysis` (fact-analysis).

- **Accurate and complete** — every fact traces to the record with a cite. Include all legally significant facts, **including unfavorable ones** (predictive: completeness; persuasive: you may de-emphasize but not omit or misstate a material fact).
- **Facts, not legal conclusions** — write "driving 50 in a 25 while texting," not "negligent." No argument in the facts (especially predictive); persuasion comes from *selection, sequence, and emphasis* of true facts.
- **Predictive (memo):** objective, complete, neutral; flag disputes ("X testified…; Y denies it"); chronological or topical.
- **Persuasive (brief):** lead with and develop favorable facts (concrete detail); handle unfavorable facts fairly but without spotlight; sound the theme; tell a story (protagonist, conflict, stakes, the resolution the law should supply) — all true to the record; favorable facts in positions of emphasis.

## Point Headings (persuasive)

In a brief, the headings are the argument's skeleton and its table of contents — a judge often reads them first. Build them **before** the prose, from the proof skeleton.

- A point heading is a **complete sentence** stating a **conclusion + its reason** ("The court should grant summary judgment because the undisputed evidence shows no duty to warn") — not a topic ("The Duty Element").
- **Hierarchy mirrors the proof** — each umbrella conclusion = a main heading; each sub-conclusion/element = a subheading that, with its siblings, establishes the main heading.
- **Order** by strength/logic (strongest/dispositive first, affirmative before defensive); **parallel** in form at each level.
- **TOC test:** extract the headings alone and read them top to bottom — they should state the conclusion on every point, give the reason, and read as a complete, persuasive argument with no gaps or overlap. If they don't argue the case by themselves, rewrite.

(Predictive memo headings are topical/neutral — handle those with `organization.md`.)

## Standard of Review (and the governing motion standard)

The standard of review sets how much deference the reviewing court gives the decision below — and often decides the appeal before the merits. For a trial-court motion, the parallel is the **governing motion standard** (the test the motion is decided under).

- **Match the standard to the kind of question, and cite it** (a retrieved case/rule, pincited). Per issue.
- **Appellate standards (general — confirm the jurisdiction's exact rule):**

| Standard | Applies to | Deference |
|---|---|---|
| **De novo** | Pure questions of law; interpretation; many SJ/dismissal rulings on appeal. | None. Best for an appellant on a legal issue. |
| **Clearly erroneous** | A judge's findings of fact. | High. |
| **Sufficiency of the evidence** | Jury findings/verdicts. | Very high. |
| **Abuse of discretion** | Evidentiary rulings, case management, many remedies. | High — but a discretionary ruling premised on a legal error gets de novo as to that premise. |
| **Mixed questions** | Law applied to fact. | Varies — argue toward the favorable end. |
| **Administrative** | Agency action/factfinding. | Per the governing statute (substantial evidence / arbitrary-and-capricious). |

- **Common motion standards:** motion to dismiss (well-pleaded facts taken as true; plausible/sufficient claim?); summary judgment (no genuine dispute of material fact; movant entitled to judgment as a matter of law; evidence viewed favorably to the non-movant); JMOL/directed verdict (no legally sufficient basis for a reasonable factfinder); preliminary injunction (the jurisdiction's multi-factor test).
- **Use it strategically** — foreground a favorable standard; work honestly within an unfavorable one (recharacterize a "mixed" question toward the favorable standard where the law supports it; attack a discretionary ruling's legal premise de novo). State the standard before the merits so the court reads your argument through the right lens.
