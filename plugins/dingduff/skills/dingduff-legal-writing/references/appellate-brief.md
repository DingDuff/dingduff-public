# Appellate Brief (Persuasive, Appellate)

A persuasive brief to a reviewing court. The appeal differs from a trial motion in three ways that shape everything: **the standard of review governs each issue, the record is closed and must be cited, and issues generally must have been preserved below.** Draft from the proof skeleton produced by `dingduff-legal-analysis` (persuasive ordering).

## Appellate discipline

- **Standard of review first** — each issue's standard determines what you must show; brief every issue through it. See `components.md`.
- **Preservation** — argue only issues preserved below (raised, ruled on, not waived); if unpreserved, address the plain-error/forfeiture posture honestly. Confirm against the record.
- **Record cites, no new facts** — every factual assertion cites the record; the court won't consider facts outside it.
- **Candor and a sound analysis** — disclose adverse controlling authority; skeleton before prose.
- **Local rules are strict** — cover, word/page limits, required sections, certificates. Defer to the governing court's rules.

## Anatomy (defer to the court's rules)

1. **Cover page** — caption, court, parties, counsel.
2. **Disclosure statement** (corporate/party) where required.
3. **Table of Contents** — doubles as the argument outline; your point headings appear here, so make them argue.
4. **Table of Authorities** — cases, statutes, other, with pages.
5. **Jurisdictional statement** — basis for jurisdiction below and on appeal; finality/timeliness.
6. **Statement of the Issues** — one per issue, framed persuasively. See `components.md`.
7. **Statement of the Case** — persuasive, record-cited facts + procedural history. See `components.md`.
8. **Standard of Review** — per issue, with authority.
9. **Summary of the Argument** — a tight, stand-alone synopsis (not a TOC in prose); the court often reads it first.
10. **Argument** — organized by point headings; each point a conclusion-first proof from the skeleton, briefed through its standard of review, confronting the opponent and adverse authority.
11. **Conclusion** — the precise relief sought (reverse, affirm, remand, vacate).
12. **Signature**, **certificates**, **addendum/appendix** as required.

## Posture

- **Appellant** — show reversible error under the applicable standard (preserved and harmful, not harmless).
- **Appellee** — show no error, or harmless error; lean on deferential standards.
- **Reply** — answer the appellee without repeating the opening; hold your theme.

## Workflow

Analyze (`dingduff-legal-analysis`, persuasive; confirm preservation, set standards of review) → draft the parts from the skeleton (issues, statement of the case, point headings, per-issue standards — `components.md`) → summary of argument → organize (`organization.md`) → persuasion pass (`persuasion.md`, audience = appellate panel) → clarity edit (`clarity.md`) → cite-check & format.

## Self-check

Each issue briefed through its standard of review; preservation confirmed; every fact record-cited (none outside it); adverse controlling authority addressed; issues few and strong; point headings argue the case in the TOC; summary of argument stands alone; relief precise; court's rules satisfied; citations checked.

## Going further (complex / high-court appeals)

Follow the "Going further" guidance in `dingduff-legal-analysis`. **Brief the rule's consequences** — a reviewing court cares about the rule it announces; argue your rule is correct, administrable, and produces sound consequences (and the opponent's does not), marshaling policy and any split. **Full counter-briefing** — build the opponent's brief and answer it pre-emptively; plan the reply. **Preservation/record, exhaustively** — map each pressed issue to where it was preserved and pincite every factual assertion.
