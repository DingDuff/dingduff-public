# Letters — Demand and Client Advice

Two correspondence types with opposite audiences. Both rest on analysis from `dingduff-legal-analysis`.

---

## Demand Letter (persuasive, to an adverse party)

Correspondence to an **adverse** reader — the opposing party or their counsel — to get something done (payment, cure, performance, cessation, preservation) without litigation, or to set up favorable settlement. It can be **used against your client later**, so accuracy and restraint matter as much as force.

### Accuracy, ethics, and protecting the client
- **Every factual and legal assertion accurate** — an overstatement becomes the opponent's exhibit. Claim only what the facts and law support.
- **Ethical limits** — don't threaten criminal prosecution (or disciplinary/administrative action) to gain civil advantage where prohibited; don't make threats you can't lawfully carry out; meet any regulated-demand requirements (e.g., consumer-debt collection). When in doubt, flag for the attorney.
- **Protect the client** — avoid admissions; reserve all rights; consider whether to mark it a settlement communication (and the consequences) under the applicable rules.
- **Don't overstate the legal position** — credibility persuades; bluster invites a dismissive response and undercuts you if you sue.

### Anatomy
1. **Sender / matter** — who you represent, regarding what; representation notice.
2. **Facts** — concise, accurate; firm, not inflammatory.
3. **Legal basis** — the claim/right supporting the demand, stated clearly (citation light).
4. **The demand** — exactly what you require (amount, action, forbearance); specific and unambiguous.
5. **Deadline** — clear and reasonable.
6. **Consequences** — a credible, lawful consequence of non-compliance (not an idle or improper threat).
7. **Reservation of rights** and professional close.

### Tone/strategy
Firm, professional, credible; make compliance the rational, easy choice; calibrate to the relationship and goal (sometimes a measured letter serves the client better than scorched earth — note the strategic choice for the attorney). Run the persuasion pass with the audience = the opponent (frame around their risk and cost), then the clarity pass.

## Client Advice Letter (predictive, to the client)

Delivers analysis to the **client** — usually a non-lawyer making a decision. Predictive and objective like a memo, but written for a different reader: bottom line first, plain language, a clear recommendation, honest caveats.

### Clear, accurate, honest about uncertainty
- **Plain language** — translate the law; define any necessary term. Comprehension, not a display of analysis.
- **Accurate — don't oversimplify into wrong.** Where the law is uncertain, say so.
- **Bottom line first**, then explain.
- **State assumptions and caveats** — the facts assumed, information still needed, limits of the advice.
- **A clear recommendation** (or well-framed options) — the client is deciding; give them something to decide with, while leaving the decision to them.
- **Privilege/confidentiality** — treat and mark as a privileged attorney-client communication.

### Anatomy
1. **Opening** — purpose and the question(s) asked.
2. **Bottom line / short answer** — up front, plain, with a realistic confidence level.
3. **Facts and assumptions** — what the advice rests on (invite correction).
4. **Explanation** — the law and how it applies, accessibly; not a brief; citations minimal.
5. **Options and risks** — realistic choices and practical upside/downside.
6. **Recommendation / next steps** — what you advise, what happens next, what you need from the client.
7. **Caveats and close** — limits, information still needed, professional sign-off.

### Tone
Warm, direct, respectful of the client's intelligence without assuming legal knowledge; manage expectations honestly; concrete and practical. Run the clarity pass tuned hard toward plain language and short sentences (this reader is not a lawyer).
