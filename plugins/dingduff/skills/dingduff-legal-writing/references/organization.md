# Large-Scale Organization

The bridge from a proof skeleton to a navigable document. `dingduff-legal-analysis` decides the *logical* order of proof; this turns that into a structure a busy reader can follow at a glance — and, run in reverse, diagnoses a draft whose structure has drifted. Legal readers skim: they read the first sentence of each paragraph, the headings, and the roadmap.

## Principles

- **Conclusion-first at every level** — document, section, paragraph each open with their point.
- **Structure must match the proof** — don't reorganize in a way that splits rule proof from its application or otherwise breaks the logical skeleton.
- **One unit, one idea** — if you can't name a paragraph's single point, it has two or none.

## Steps

1. **Macro-sequence** — lay out the sections in the skeleton's order (threshold/dispositive first; then logic for a memo, strength for a brief). Each top-level proof unit is a section; sub-conclusions are subsections.
2. **Roadmap and umbrellas** — open the Discussion/Argument with a few sentences giving the conclusion, the issues, and the order. Before a multi-part analysis, state the overall conclusion and list the parts, then take them in order.
3. **Headings** — the visible skeleton the reader navigates by. Predictive: topical/neutral. Persuasive: full-sentence conclusion-bearing point headings (see `components.md`). Parallel in form, mutually exclusive in content.
4. **Topic sentences** — open every paragraph with its point, linked to the section's. A reader who reads only topic sentences should get the whole argument in miniature. Keep citations and qualifiers out of the topic-sentence position.
5. **Transitions** — connect units so the logic moves. Prefer *substantive* transitions that state the relationship ("Because the notice was defective, the second requirement also fails…") over bare connectives; avoid empty filler ("It is important to note that…").

## Reverse-outline check (for an existing draft)

Extract the first sentence of every paragraph and read them in sequence — they should form a coherent outline. If not: a topic sentence that doesn't state its point → rewrite; two paragraphs making the same point → merge; a point in two places → consolidate; a paragraph that doesn't fit its section → move or cut; a logical gap between consecutive points → add the missing step. If the gap is *logical* (not presentational), the hole is in the analysis — send it back to `dingduff-legal-analysis`, not a transition.

## Self-check

Conclusion first at document, section, and paragraph level; roadmap previews conclusion/issues/order; headings (read alone) convey the structure; every paragraph opens with a point; transitions state real relationships; structure still matches the logical skeleton.
