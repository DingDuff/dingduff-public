# Signals, Structure, and Parentheticals

## Where citations go and how they punctuate

In a brief, citations sit in the body text, right after the proposition they support. (Footnotes are used only where local rules allow or require them.) A citation appears either as a citation sentence or a citation clause.

- **Citation sentence** — supports the **entire** preceding sentence. It stands on its own, begins with a capital letter, and ends with a period. Multiple authorities within it are separated by semicolons.
  > A complaint must allege facts that make the claim plausible, not merely possible. *Okafor v. Summit Indus., Inc.*, 305 F. Supp. 3d 410, 415 (S.D. Tex. 2018); *Vasquez v. Coastal Freight Co.*, 640 F.3d 200, 205 (9th Cir. 2011).
- **Citation clause** — supports only **part** of a sentence. It is set off by commas, sits next to the words it supports, does not begin with a capital (unless it starts with a source that is always capitalized), and does not end with a period unless it is the last clause in the sentence.
  > The duty runs to foreseeable plaintiffs, *see Marsh v. Cedar Valley Hosp.*, 215 S.W.3d 700, 705 (Tex. App. 2006), but not to purely economic losses, *see Whitman v. Harbor Mut. Ins. Co.*, 431 P.3d 220, 224 (Cal. 2018).

## Introductory signals

A signal tells the reader how the cited authority relates to your point. Signals are italicized. Capitalize a signal that begins a citation sentence; lowercase it when it begins a citation clause.

| Signal | What it tells the reader |
|---|---|
| *[no signal]* | Use no signal when the cited source itself states the point, supplies a quotation you just used, or was named in your sentence. |
| *e.g.,* | The point is stated by many sources; you are giving one or a few as representative samples rather than an exhaustive list. |
| *Accord* | Use after a cited or quoted authority to add others that say the same thing — handy when two jurisdictions agree. |
| *See* | The source backs the point through a step of reasoning rather than saying it outright. |
| *See also* | An extra source that helps, offered on top of authority you have already given. |
| *Cf.* | The source decides a related but distinct question in a way that lends support; spell out the connection in a parenthetical. |
| *Compare … with …* | Set two (or two sets of) authorities side by side because the contrast itself proves your point. |
| *Contra* | The opposite of no-signal: the source flatly states the reverse of your point. |
| *But see* | The source cuts against your point through reasoning rather than stating the opposite outright. |
| *But cf.* | The source supports a proposition that runs contrary by analogy; explain the analogy in a parenthetical. |
| *See generally* | Useful background or context rather than direct support. |

You can attach *e.g.* to *see* or *but see*: *See, e.g.,* … and *But see, e.g.,* …

## Order of signals

When one citation sentence uses more than one signal, the signals (with their authorities) appear in the order in the table above — support signals first, then comparison, then contradiction, then background. Signals of different "types" are normally placed in **separate citation sentences** rather than strung together.

## Order of authorities within a signal

Separate authorities under one signal with semicolons. List a markedly more authoritative or helpful source first; otherwise use this order:

1. Constitutions (federal, then state, then foreign).
2. Statutes (federal, then state, then foreign).
3. Treaties and other international agreements.
4. Cases, in descending order of authority — **federal first**: U.S. Supreme Court, then the courts of appeals (treated together as one group, not ordered by circuit number), then the district courts; **then state cases**, alphabetically by state, and within each state by court rank, highest first. Within any one of these groups, order the cases most recent first.
5. Legislative materials (bills, then hearings, then reports, then debates).
6. Administrative materials (executive orders, then regulations, then proposed rules).
7. Court documents.
8. Secondary materials (restatements, then books, then articles, then internet sources).

Within a single court level, order cases from most recent to oldest.

## Explanatory parentheticals

A parenthetical after a citation explains the authority's relevance. Begin it with a present participle (*holding*, *finding*, *noting*, *explaining*, *rejecting*, *concluding*) — unless the parenthetical is a full quoted sentence or a very short phrase. Do not capitalize the participle or end with a period, unless the parenthetical is a complete quoted sentence.

- *See Vasquez v. Coastal Freight Co.*, 640 F.3d 200, 206 (9th Cir. 2011) (explaining that the disclaimer failed because it was buried in fine print and never separately acknowledged by the buyer).
- Quoted full sentence: *Okafor v. Summit Indus., Inc.*, 305 F. Supp. 3d 410, 418 (S.D. Tex. 2018) ("A warranty disclaimer that a reasonable buyer would never notice is no disclaimer at all.").

When a citation needs more than one parenthetical, order them: weight/posture, then a "quoting"/"citing" parenthetical, then the explanatory parenthetical, then any "(citation omitted)" / "(emphasis added)" notes — and an explanatory parenthetical about the case comes before subsequent history. When the non-substantive cleanup notes start to pile up, you can collapse them into a single "(cleaned up)" parenthetical — see `short-forms-and-quotations.md`.
