# Short Forms and Quotations

## Short forms generally

After a full citation, use a short form for every later reference — provided (1) the reader can tell which authority you mean, (2) the full citation is in the same general discussion, and (3) the reader can find the full citation without trouble. In a footnoted document, a common "preceding five" norm lets you keep using a short form as long as the full citation appears within the previous five footnotes; the convention is not absolute, so give a fresh full cite whenever a short form would be ambiguous.

### *id.*

*id.* refers to the **immediately preceding** authority. Capitalize it (*Id.*) only when it begins a citation sentence; the period is part of the word, and the italics run under it.

- Same page as the preceding cite: *Id.*
- Different page: *Id.* at 418.
- Different section of the same statute/title: *Id.* § 1404.

Use *id.* only when the immediately preceding citation points to a single authority. If the preceding citation strung together several authorities, use a different short form instead.

### Case short forms

Once a case has a full citation, refer to it later by a distinctive party name + the volume and reporter + "at" + the pincite. Given a full citation of *Whitman v. Harbor Mut. Ins. Co.*, 431 P.3d 220 (Cal. 2018), the short forms for page 224 are all acceptable:

- *Whitman*, 431 P.3d at 224.
- 431 P.3d at 224.
- *Id.* at 224.

Choose the memorable party — not "United States," "State," "City of ___," a government agency, or another common litigant. You may shorten a long party name (*Riverside Sav. & Loan Ass'n* → *Riverside*) as long as it stays unambiguous.

With a parallel citation, the short form carries both reporters:

- Full: *Doyle v. Northgate Mfg. Co.*, 410 Mass. 88, 92, 575 N.E.2d 1100, 1103 (1991).
- Short: *Doyle*, 410 Mass. at 92, 575 N.E.2d at 1103.

### *supra* and *hereinafter*

*supra* is used for short forms of books, articles, legislative materials, and the like (not for cases, statutes, constitutions, or regulations, which use *id.* or a repeated cite). Form: author's last name, *supra*, new pincite — or, in a footnoted document, *supra* note __. Use *hereinafter* to coin a short tag for a source with a long or confusing name, declaring it in brackets at the full citation.

## Quotations

### Inline vs. block

- **Fewer than 50 words:** keep the quotation in your text, inside quotation marks.
- **50 words or more:** set it as a **block quotation** — indented on both sides, single-spaced, with no quotation marks. Quotation marks that appear inside the quoted passage stay as they were. The citation that follows a block quotation is **not** indented; it begins at the left margin on the line after the block.

Punctuation with quotation marks: commas and periods go **inside** the closing quotation mark; other punctuation goes inside only when it is part of the quoted material.

A block quotation in practice looks like this — the passage is indented and unquoted, and the citation sits flush left on the next line:

> The implied warranty of merchantability attaches by operation of law to every sale by a merchant of goods of that kind. A seller who wishes to disclaim it must do so in language the buyer would actually see and understand; a disclaimer hidden among boilerplate, in type no larger than the surrounding text, does not satisfy that standard and will not be enforced.

*Okafor v. Summit Indus., Inc.*, 305 F. Supp. 3d 410, 418 (S.D. Tex. 2018). The court then turned to damages. *Id.* at 421.

### Alterations

- **Changed capitalization:** bracket the changed letter — "[T]he carrier is liable for any loss." / "the rule, [t]hough longstanding, no longer controls."
- **Substituted or inserted words:** put them in brackets — the court held that "[the buyer] had no duty to inspect [the goods] before acceptance."
- **Omitted letters from a word:** empty brackets — quote "the carrier's 'duty[ ]'" where the original read "duties."
- **Error in the original:** mark it with "[sic]" and otherwise leave it — the contract stated that the goods "was [sic] delivered on time."
- **Emphasis you added:** note it after the citation — (emphasis added). Emphasis that was in the original need not be noted; if you note an alteration of the quoted text, add (alteration in original).

### Omissions (ellipses)

Show an omission with an ellipsis: three periods, each separated by a space, with a space before the first and after the last — `. . .` 

- Do **not** begin a quotation with an ellipsis; just start where you mean to.
- When you omit the end of a quoted sentence (or omit material after a sentence ends), keep that sentence's final punctuation, so the result is four dots — `. . . .`
- Do not mark the omission of a citation or footnote-call number that follows the last word you quote; instead add (citation omitted) after the citation.

Example pulling several rules together:

> "[A]n employer may not retaliate against an employee . . . who reports a suspected violation in *good faith*, even when the violation is never proven."

*Vasquez v. Coastal Freight Co.*, 640 F.3d 200, 207 (9th Cir. 2011) (emphasis added) (citation omitted).

Here the bracketed "[A]" shows a capitalization change you made, the spaced dots mark words you dropped, the italics on *good faith* are emphasis you added (hence "emphasis added"), and a citation that sat in the middle of the quoted passage was removed (hence "citation omitted").

### Consolidating cleanup: "(cleaned up)"

The granular approach above flags every non-substantive edit with its own parenthetical, so a single citation can trail a string of them — "(citation omitted) (internal quotation marks omitted) (alteration in original)" — which is the very clutter that hurts readability. A now-widely-used shortcut folds all of that into one parenthetical: **(cleaned up)**.

Putting **(cleaned up)** after a citation tells the reader that, purely for readability, you have stripped out extraneous non-substantive matter from the quotation — internal quotation marks, brackets, ellipses, internal citations, and footnote-call numbers — and may have adjusted capitalization, without marking each change individually. It also lets you attribute the language directly to the opinion you are citing even when that opinion was itself quoting an earlier source, so you can drop the nested "quoting ... (quoting ...)" chain. In exchange, you take responsibility for confirming that the streamlined quotation stays faithful to the original's meaning.

Use it honestly and within limits:

- Only for **non-substantive** changes that aid readability — it can never disguise an edit that changes meaning.
- It does **not** absorb emphasis you added; still append "(emphasis added)" separately, because added emphasis is a substantive change.
- Verify the quotation against the original source before relying on it; the shortcut shifts the accuracy burden onto you.

The same quotation, the long way and the consolidated way:

> "The warranty attaches by operation of law, and a disclaimer the buyer would never see does not defeat it." *Okafor v. Summit Indus., Inc.*, 305 F. Supp. 3d 410, 418 (S.D. Tex. 2018) (internal quotation marks omitted) (citation omitted) (alteration in original).

> "The warranty attaches by operation of law, and a disclaimer the buyer would never see does not defeat it." *Okafor v. Summit Indus., Inc.*, 305 F. Supp. 3d 410, 418 (S.D. Tex. 2018) (cleaned up).

A note on the label. This guide uses **(cleaned up)**, the convention also adopted by *The Indigo Book*'s second edition. The original 2016 *Indigo Book* predates the parenthetical (it was proposed by Jack Metzler in 2017), but "(cleaned up)" has since become the prevailing practitioner form — every federal circuit has used it and it has appeared in a unanimous U.S. Supreme Court opinion. The latest edition of *The Bluebook* recognizes a similar consolidation under a different label, "(citation modified)," but that form is far less common in filings; this guide follows *The Indigo Book* and prevailing practice. Both the consolidated and the granular forms are optional — follow your court's convention if it has one, and you may always keep the individual cleanup parentheticals instead.
