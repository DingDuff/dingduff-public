# Secondary Sources and Court-Filed Documents

In court-document style, secondary sources use ordinary roman type for the author's name and the journal/publisher name; only the **title of the work** is italicized. (The large-and-small-caps of academic style are not used.) The author names, titles, volumes, and pages in the examples below are illustrative — they show form, not real publications.

## Books, treatises, and other non-periodic works

Parts: an optional volume number, the author(s), the italicized title, the pincite, and a parenthetical with the year (plus the editor and edition if applicable).

- Single author: Helena R. Marsh, *The Law of Maritime Liens* 142 (2d ed. 2019).
- Two authors — join with `&`: Daniel P. Okafor & Lucia M. Vasquez, *Principles of Insurance Coverage* 88 (2021).
- Three or more authors — list the first and add "et al.": James A. Calloway et al., *Federal Pretrial Practice* 305 (4th ed. 2018).
- Multi-volume treatise (volume number first; cite by section): 3 Robert E. Whitman, *A Treatise on Commercial Transactions* § 17.4 (3d ed. 2016).
- Edited collection: *Essays on Modern Tort Theory* 210 (Helena R. Marsh ed., 2015).

Give the author's full name as it appears in the publication. Add the edition when there is more than one (2d ed., 3d ed., rev. ed.).

### Encyclopedias and dictionaries

- 32 Am. Jur. 2d *Insurance* § 14 (2020); 17A C.J.S. *Contracts* § 312 (2021).
- Dictionary entry: *Proximate Cause*, Black's Law Dictionary (11th ed. 2019).

## Periodical articles

### Consecutively paginated journals (law reviews)

Parts: author(s), italicized article title, volume, abbreviated journal name, first page of the article, pincite, and the year in parentheses.

Pattern: `<Author>, <Title>, <Volume> <Journal> <First Page>, <Pincite> (<Year>).`

- Lucia M. Vasquez, *Rethinking the Economic Loss Rule*, 95 Tex. L. Rev. 1203, 1219 (2017).
- Daniel P. Okafor & Helena R. Marsh, *The Limits of Vicarious Liability*, 130 Harv. L. Rev. 880, 902 (2017).

Student-written pieces add the designation after the author: James A. Calloway, Note, *Reviving the Public Trust Doctrine*, 118 Colum. L. Rev. 455, 478 n.12 (2018). Use the journal abbreviations in the standard periodicals list.

### Non-consecutively paginated journals and magazines

Give the date and use "at" before the page.

- Helena R. Marsh, *The New Privacy Wars*, The Atlantic, June 2022, at 44.

### Newspapers

- Daniel P. Okafor, *City Council Approves Zoning Overhaul*, N.Y. Times, Mar. 3, 2022, at A1.

## Short forms for secondary sources

Use *id.* for the immediately preceding authority. Otherwise use *supra*: the author's last name, then *supra* (italicized up to but not including the comma), then the new pincite. If the document cites more than one work by the same author, add a short title.

- *Id.* at 204–05.
- Vasquez, *supra*, at 1219.
- Marsh, *The New Privacy Wars*, *supra*, at 46.

In a document that uses footnotes, *supra* points to the footnote of the full citation: Vasquez, *supra* note 12, at 1219.

## Documents filed in the same matter

For a document filed in the same case, abbreviate the document's title, then give the pincite directly (no "at" before a plain page number), the date if needed, and the ECF/docket number where helpful. Use `¶`/`¶¶` for paragraphs. Citations to court documents may be enclosed in parentheses.

- Compl. ¶ 12.
- Answer ¶ 4.
- Pl.'s Mot. Summ. J. 8.
- Vasquez Decl. ¶¶ 5–7.
- Okafor Dep. 112:4–14.
- (Hr'g Tr. 33:9–18.)
- Ex. C, at 2. (Use "at" when a bare number would be ambiguous.)

For the official record on appeal, abbreviate "Record" to "R.": R. at 112. Do not abbreviate where doing so would confuse the reader (and do not abbreviate document titles when they appear in a textual sentence — write "Plaintiff's Motion for Summary Judgment," not "Pl.'s Mot. Summ. J.," in running prose).

To cite a document filed in a **different** case, cite it the way you would cite that case's filing, identifying the case.
