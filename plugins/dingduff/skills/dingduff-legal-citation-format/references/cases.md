# Cases

A full case citation has up to five parts, in this order:

`<Case Name>, <Volume> <Reporter> <First Page>, <Pincite> (<Court> <Year>) (<parentheticals>)<, history>.`

Example with every part:

> *Vasquez v. Summit Indus., Inc.*, 712 F.3d 320, 327 (9th Cir. 2013) (holding that the warranty disclaimer was unenforceable), *rev'd en banc*, 760 F.3d 901 (9th Cir. 2015).

Build the full citation the first time the case appears; use a short form afterward (see `short-forms-and-quotations.md`).

> The party names, volume numbers, and page numbers in the examples throughout this skill are illustrative — they demonstrate form, not real holdings. Apply the patterns to the actual authority you are citing.

## 1. Case name

Italicize the entire case name **up to but not including** the comma that follows it. The *v.* is italicized; the trailing comma is not.

Rules for shaping the name:

- **One party per side.** Keep only the first-listed party on each side of the *v.* Drop everything after it.
  - *Northgate Mfg. Co. v. Coastal Freight Co.* (not "Northgate Manufacturing Company, Inc., Plaintiff, v. Coastal Freight Company and Three Rivers Shipping, LLC, Defendants").
- **Individuals: surname only.** *Alvarez v. Whitman* (not "Maria Elena Alvarez v. Thomas J. Whitman").
- **Drop "et al.," "a.k.a.," and similar.** Omit indicators of additional parties and alternative names — *Okafor v. Renner*, even if the caption lists several plaintiffs.
- **Procedural phrases.** Shorten "in the matter of," "petition of," and the like to *In re*; shorten "on the relation of," "on behalf of," and the like to *ex rel.* When there are adversary parties on both sides, drop every procedural phrase **except** *ex rel.*
  - *In re Calloway*; *Ex parte Whitman*; *State ex rel. Alvarez v. Doyle*.
- **"The."** Omit "The" as the first word of a party's name (unless it is part of the name of the object in an in rem action).
- **"United States."** Never abbreviate it when it is a party. *United States v. Renner* (not "U.S. v. Renner"). Use "United States," not "United States of America."
- **"People of," "State of," "Commonwealth of."** Omit these phrases — *unless* you are citing a court located in that state, in which case keep only *People*, *State*, or *Commonwealth*.
  - *Alvarez v. Colorado* (citing another court), but *Alvarez v. State*, 109 P.3d 224 (Colo. 2018) (citing a Colorado court).
- **"City of," "Town of," "Village of."** Keep a locality phrase only when it is the **first** part of a party's name; otherwise drop it. *Renner v. City of Lawton*, but *City of Lawton v. Renner*.
- **Commissioner of Internal Revenue.** Cite simply as *Commissioner* (abbreviated *Comm'r* in a citation). *Pinnacle Techs. v. Comm'r*.
- **Commonly recognized organizations.** Use the widely recognized initials for well-known entities — *... v. FCC*, *... v. SEC*, *... v. NAACP*, *Holloway v. NLRB* — rather than spelling the agency out.
- **Abbreviate words in a citation clause** using the standard case-name abbreviations (see `abbreviations.md`); abbreviate states and other geographic units unless the unit is itself a named party. You may also abbreviate any word of eight or more letters if it saves substantial space and stays unambiguous.
  - *Riverside Sav. & Loan Ass'n v. Pinnacle Techs., Inc.*
- **Distinctive case identifiers.** When a case has several dispositions, you may add an italicized identifier the first time and reuse it later: *Calloway v. Brightline Energy Corp. (Calloway II)*, ... . When a case is widely known by a popular name, give the reported name in full and add the popular name in an italicized parenthetical: *Northgate Mfg. Co. v. Coastal Freight Co. (The Container Case)*, ... .

### Case name inside a textual sentence

When the case name is a grammatical part of your sentence (a "textual sentence") rather than sitting in a citation clause, do **not** abbreviate it — with two narrow exceptions: you may still abbreviate widely known acronyms, and you may always abbreviate these eight words: **&, Ass'n, Bros., Co., Corp., Inc., Ltd., and No.** Spell out everything else (Department, District, United States, geographic names, etc.).

- Textual: In *Northgate Manufacturing Co. v. City of Lawton*, 818 F.3d 470 (7th Cir. 2016), the court applied the diminution-in-value rule.
- Citation clause: *... applied the rule. Northgate Mfg. Co. v. City of Lawton*, 818 F.3d 470 (7th Cir. 2016).

On the first textual mention, follow the case name with the rest of the full citation, set off by commas, as shown above. On later references, name just the distinctive party — here *Northgate*, not *City of Lawton* (a city is a common litigant and makes a poor short name).

## 2. Reporter and pinpoint citation

Cite a reporter by listing (1) the volume number, (2) the abbreviated reporter name, and (3) the page on which the opinion begins. Then add the **pincite** — the exact page(s) supporting your point — after a comma.

- *Alvarez v. Whitman*, 245 F.3d 110, 114 (5th Cir. 2001).

Pincite mechanics:

- **Page range:** give first and last page separated by an en dash; for numbers of three or more digits, drop repeated leading digits but keep the last two — 114–16, 1120–25, 102–06.
- **Non-consecutive pages:** list each, separated by commas — 110, 114–16, 132. If the material spans more pages than is practical to list, use *passim*.
- **First page is the pincite:** repeat the number — *Renner v. Pinnacle Techs., Inc.*, 488 F.3d 415, 415 (6th Cir. 2007).
- **Footnotes:** give the page, then `n.` and the footnote number with no internal space — 226 n.4. Multiple footnotes: nn.4–6.

Which reporter to cite: cite the official reporter where one is designated; otherwise the regional/commercial reporter. For the U.S. Supreme Court, cite the United States Reports (U.S.) when the opinion appears there; otherwise the Supreme Court Reporter (S. Ct.). See `abbreviations.md` for the reporter list.

**Public-domain (media-neutral) citations.** Many states have adopted a public-domain or media-neutral citation — a vendor-independent cite keyed to the case, year, and paragraph numbers rather than to a printed reporter (e.g., `Whitman v. Doyle, 2019 ND 14, ¶ 7`). *The Indigo Book* encourages using the public-domain citation where a jurisdiction has one, and giving a **parallel citation** to the regional reporter as well when possible.

**Parallel citations in state-court filings.** State local rules often require a parallel citation — the official state reporter first, then the regional/state-specific reporter — with one pincite per reporter. When the official reporter's title already names the state or court, do not repeat it in the parenthetical.

- *Doyle v. Northgate Mfg. Co.*, 410 Mass. 88, 92, 575 N.E.2d 1100, 1103 (1991).

## 3. Court and year

In the parenthetical, give the deciding court, then the year of decision.

- **U.S. Supreme Court:** no court abbreviation — only the year. *Alvarez v. United States*, 571 U.S. 320, 331 (2014).
- **A state's highest court:** omit the court name, but identify the state. When you cite a regional reporter (which never names a single state), put the state in the parenthetical: *Whitman v. Harbor Mut. Ins. Co.*, 431 P.3d 220, 224 (Cal. 2018). Drop the state only when you cite the state's own official reporter, whose title already names it — then give the year alone, e.g., *Doyle v. Northgate Mfg. Co.*, 410 Mass. 88, 92 (1991).
- **Federal court of appeals:** circuit + year. *Vasquez v. Coastal Freight Co.*, 640 F.3d 200, 207 (9th Cir. 2011).
- **Federal district court:** district + year. *Okafor v. Summit Indus., Inc.*, 305 F. Supp. 3d 410, 418 (S.D. Tex. 2018).
- **State intermediate court:** *Marsh v. Cedar Valley Hosp.*, 215 S.W.3d 700, 705 (Tex. App. 2006); other forms look like *... (Cal. Ct. App. 2010)*, *... (N.Y. App. Div. 2009)*. Do **not** include the department or district of an intermediate state court in the parenthetical.

Spacing in court abbreviations: close up adjacent single capitals (S.D.N.Y., D.C. Cir.), but keep a space between a single capital and a longer abbreviation (D. Mass., S.D. Cal., D. Conn.). See `abbreviations.md`.

## 4. Weight of authority and explanatory parentheticals

Additional parentheticals follow the date parenthetical, grouped as:

1. **Weight / posture:** (en banc), (per curiam), (plurality opinion), (5–4 decision).
2. **Separate opinion:** (Tann, J., dissenting), (Renner, J., concurring in the judgment).
3. **Explanatory parenthetical:** a present-participle phrase or a quotation showing why the case matters.

When several parentheticals (and any quotation-cleanup notes) stack up on one citation, follow the single canonical sequence set out in `signals-and-structure.md` (Indigo R36), which controls their exact order.

> *Calloway v. Brightline Energy Corp.*, 812 F.3d 455, 461 (7th Cir. 2016) (en banc) (Tann, J., dissenting) ("The majority's rule rewards precisely the gamesmanship our precedent was meant to deter.").

Explanatory parentheticals usually begin with a present participle — *holding*, *finding*, *noting*, *explaining*, *rejecting*, *concluding* — and are not capitalized and take no closing period, unless the parenthetical is itself a full quoted sentence. An explanatory parenthetical about the case comes **before** any subsequent-history phrase.

## 5. Prior and subsequent history

Append history after the parentheticals, set off by a comma, with the connecting phrase italicized.

- *Marsh v. Cedar Valley Hosp.*, 215 S.W.3d 700 (Tex. App. 2006), *aff'd*, 248 S.W.3d 220 (Tex. 2008).
- Name change on appeal — use *sub nom.*: *Doyle v. Lakeshore Transit Auth.*, 512 F.3d 330 (6th Cir. 2008), *rev'd sub nom. Lakeshore Transit Auth. v. Doyle*, 559 U.S. 410 (2010).

Drop a *cert. denied* notation once the Supreme Court's denial of certiorari is **more than two years in the past** (the same goes for a denial of rehearing), unless the denial is itself significant to your point. Do not give a new case name when the parties are simply reversed on appeal. Common history phrases (all italicized): *aff'd*, *aff'g*, *rev'd*, *rev'd on other grounds*, *rev'g*, *vacated*, *remanded*, *modified*, *cert. denied*, *cert. granted*, *reh'g denied*, *overruled by*, *abrogated by*, *superseded by statute*.

## 6. Unreported and electronic cases

When a case is not in a reporter, cite the electronic database or slip opinion.

- **Electronic database** (Westlaw / Lexis): case name, docket number, database identifier and report number, star-page pincite, then court and full date.
  - *Calloway v. Meridian Logistics, Inc.*, No. 22-cv-0451, 2023 WL 1234567, at *3 (N.D. Ill. Feb. 14, 2023).
  - *Whitman v. Brightline Energy Corp.*, No. 21-3389, 2022 U.S. App. LEXIS 9981, at *5 (7th Cir. Apr. 8, 2022).
- **Slip opinion** (no database): docket number, court, and full date of the most recent major disposition.
  - *Renner v. Coastal Freight Co.*, No. 20-1187, slip op. at 4 (S.D.N.Y. Jan. 9, 2021).
- **Opinion available only on a court's website:** add the URL at the end.
  - *Okafor v. Summit Indus., Inc.*, No. 2021-0345, slip op. at 2 (Del. Ch. Mar. 3, 2021), https://courts.delaware.gov/opinions.

Reproduce a docket number exactly as the court assigns it; the lead-in (No., Civ. A., etc.) may be dropped on later references.
