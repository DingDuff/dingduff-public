# Issue Framing

Method for the first step of legal analysis: turn a situation into a clean, ordered set of **framed issues** plus a **theory of the case**. Get this wrong and everything downstream is well-built proof of the wrong thing.

Output: an ordered list of issues, each stated precisely, classified by the kind of dispute it is, and framed from the decision-maker's point of view — plus a one-line theory/theme and a note on the audience and the controlling constraint (standard of review, burden).

## Issues come from law + facts in hand, not invention

An issue is a genuine legal question the *retrieved* law and the *actual* facts raise — never a plausible question generated from memory. Each must trace to a real claim/defense element, a real ambiguity in retrieved authority, or a real feature of the facts. Don't silently drop an element because it seems easy — name it, even if you later mark it undisputed. If you suspect an issue but lack the law or facts to confirm it, flag it and escalate.

## Predictive vs. persuasive

- **Predictive** (memo, advice): completeness and accuracy. Spot *every* issue the facts genuinely raise, including the ones that hurt. Rank by significance; don't discard real issues.
- **Persuasive** (brief): *select* — raise the issues that help, drop the weak and trivial, frame what remains for advantage. But never ignore a dispositive issue you must address (a jurisdictional bar, controlling adverse authority).

## Steps

1. **Spot candidates broadly** — from three sources: (a) the **elements** of each potential claim/defense/motion (each contested element is a candidate); (b) the **facts** (unusual, disputed, or near a legal line); (c) the **posture** (jurisdiction, standing, timeliness, standard of review, burden).
2. **Classify each by the kind of dispute** (this tells you where the fight is):
   - **Fact** — what happened? (a dispute about the record)
   - **Definition / characterization** — do the facts meet the rule's term? (the most common legal issue)
   - **Evaluation** — how should this be weighed? (reasonableness, materiality, good faith, balancing)
   - **Procedure / authority** — should this body decide it, and how? (jurisdiction, standard of review, remedy)
3. **Select and narrow** — persuasive: keep what wins or must be answered, combine overlapping issues, resist raising every arguable point (few matters truly turn on more than three or four). Predictive: keep all genuine issues; group sub-issues under their parent; mark which are dispositive.
4. **Frame each from the decision-maker's perspective** — state the issue as the person deciding it needs to see it (neutral for a memo, favorable-but-accurate for a brief). A good frame is concrete (names the legally significant facts), single (one question), and answerable. Fix the **audience** and the **controlling constraint** (standard of review, burden, presumption).
5. **Order** — dispositive/threshold first (jurisdiction, a complete bar, a controlling standard); then logical order (predictive) or strongest-first, affirmative-before-defensive (persuasive).
6. **Theory of the case / theme** — distill the matter to one unifying through-line (the honest center of gravity for a memo; the theme you will sound throughout for a brief). One or two sentences; everything downstream serves it.

## Idea-generation techniques (when issues won't surface)

- **Focused freewriting** — write continuously from "The real question here is…" without editing.
- **Looping** — freewrite, extract the key sentence, freewrite again from it; repeat.
- **Concept-mapping** — put the claim at center, branch to elements/parties/facts/authorities; gaps and clusters are issues.
- **The common topics** — run the facts past precedent, text/statute, policy, principle/equity, analogy, definition, history/intent; each that fires is a candidate issue.

## Self-check

Every element of every live claim/defense accounted for; each issue traces to real law + real facts; each is single, concrete, answerable; audience and controlling constraint fixed; ordering reflects posture.

## Going further (high-stakes matters)

For appellate, dispositive, formal-opinion, or novel matters:
- **Exhaustive inventory** — enumerate *every* plausible issue, including every sub-issue and the issues a careful **opponent** would raise (draft their issue list too). Select what to press only after the inventory is complete.
- **Test competing framings** — generate two or three rival framings of each significant issue (broad vs. narrow; rule-focused vs. fact-focused; yours vs. the opponent's) and choose deliberately, recording why the winner beat the alternatives (that reasoning is also your answer to the opponent's framing).
- **Classify every sub-issue** with the four-question test — mixed matters hide their real dispute at the sub-issue level.
- **Preservation/waiver** (review posture) — confirm each issue was raised and ruled on below; map it to the record; flag anything reviewable only for plain error or not at all.
- **Cross-issue interaction** — map how issues interact: does resolving A moot B? does a concession on A undermine B? what order of operations must the court follow? This drives final ordering and prevents inconsistent arguments.
