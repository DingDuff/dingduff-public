# Fact Analysis

Method for building the facts half of the analysis: find the facts that legally matter, fix their status and valence, characterize them accurately, and map each to the part of the rule it bears on. Output: the legally significant facts, each tagged with the element/factor it bears on, its valence (favorable / unfavorable / neutral), and its status (established / disputed / missing), plus a gap/disputed list. This is the bridge to the **Application** in `proof-structure.md`, and the raw material for the writing skill's statement of facts.

## Facts come from the record, never from invention

- Every fact traces to a **source in hand** — the record, the client's documents, pleadings, retrieved exhibits. Don't assume, infer beyond what the source supports, or supply a convenient fact.
- Always distinguish **established** (in evidence/undisputed), **disputed** (alleged but contested), and **missing** (needed but not in hand). Never present an alleged fact as established.
- Don't misstate or omit a material fact, even an unfavorable one. Characterization is legitimate; misrepresenting the record is not.
- Distinguish a **fact** from a **legal conclusion.** "He was negligent" is a conclusion; "he was texting while driving" is a fact. Strip conclusions back to the facts that support them.

## Steps

1. **Inventory** — pull the facts into a plain list (who/what/when/where/how, plus procedural facts), each with its source/citation. Resist analysis here.
2. **Classify by legal significance** against the rule — **determinative** (bears on an element/factor/standard), **explanatory** (context needed to follow the story), or **irrelevant** (set aside, but be sure). For each determinative fact, note **which element** it bears on.
3. **Classify by valence and status** — favorable / unfavorable / neutral (objective for a memo; relative to the client for a brief), and established / disputed / missing. Flag **gap facts** (determinative facts you lack) and **disputed facts** for follow-up.
4. **Characterize accurately** — the same fact can be described at different levels of generality and tone while staying true to the record. Predictive: neutral and precise. Persuasive: the most favorable *accurate* characterization (concrete detail for helpful facts; fair, plainer treatment for unhelpful ones). Keep the underlying fact and its characterization distinct so the line between framing and misstatement stays visible.
5. **Map facts to the rule** — for each element/factor, list the facts that satisfy it, the facts that defeat it, and any element with no fact on point (a gap or a concession). This map *is* the application skeleton — hand it to `proof-structure.md` as the **A** of each proof unit.

## Self-check

Every fact traces to a source; established/disputed/missing assigned to each determinative fact; facts distinguished from legal conclusions; each determinative fact mapped to an element and every element checked for fact support; unfavorable and disputed facts named, not buried.

## Escalation

Missing determinative facts → flag for more investigation or state the assumption explicitly. A "fact" that is really a contested legal characterization → treat it as an issue (back to `issue-framing.md` / `proof-structure.md`), not a fact. Facts that reveal a new issue → back to `issue-framing.md`.
