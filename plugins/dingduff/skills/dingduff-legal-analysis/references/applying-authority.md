# Applying Authority

Method for *using* authority in an argument (distinct from finding it). It supplies the case- and statute-level moves that become the **E** (rule proof) and **A** (application) of each proof unit. Two halves — precedent and statutes; most analyses use both.

## Honest characterization, verbatim quotation

- A case stands for what it **held** on its facts. Don't inflate **dicta** into holding or state a holding broader than the decision.
- Quotations are **verbatim** from the **retrieved** opinion/statute, with a **pincite** — never from memory or a snippet, never tidied into what you wish it said.
- Confront **adverse binding authority** — you can't win by ignoring it, and candor may require disclosure. Distinguish, limit, or show it superseded; don't pretend it isn't there.
- Apply each authority's **weight** honestly (binding vs. persuasive, court level, recency, on-point-ness).

## Using precedent

- **Establish what the case gives you** — its weight, its holding (rule applied to facts + result), and the determinative facts that produced that result.
- **Analogize** (to get the same result) — show the legally significant facts match a favorable case so the same rule yields the same outcome. The comparison must be on the facts the rule makes relevant, not superficial similarity.
- **Distinguish** (to escape an adverse case) — show a *material* difference, in a fact the rule makes decisive, so the result doesn't control. A distinction on an irrelevant fact persuades no one.
- **Rule-based vs. analogical reasoning** — argue from a synthesized rule when one is clear; argue case-to-case analogy when the rule is fuzzy and the facts do the work. Often both.
- **Deploy economically** — **parentheticals** carry a supporting case's point in a phrase (informative, present-participle: "holding that…"); **quote sparingly** (only operative language); develop a case in full only when it's your anchor or the adverse case you must distinguish.

## Statutory / textual argument

Build it in the order courts expect:

1. **Text / plain meaning first** — the words in their ordinary meaning. Most interpretation begins and ends here.
2. **Language/grammar canons (intrinsic):** *expressio unius* (listing some implies excluding others), *ejusdem generis* (a general term after a list takes the list's class), *noscitur a sociis* (a word known by its neighbors), consistent usage, defined terms.
3. **Structure (intrinsic)** — read the provision against headings, definitions, related subsections, the whole scheme.
4. **Extrinsic sources, only for genuine ambiguity** — purpose, legislative history, related statutes (*in pari materia*), agency interpretation, substantive canons (lenity, constitutional avoidance, presumption against retroactivity, remedial statutes construed broadly). Use these to resolve ambiguity, not to override clear text.

## Holding vs. dicta

**Holding** = the rule applied to the facts to reach the result; only it binds. **Dicta** = statements not necessary to the decision; persuasive at best. State a case's holding at the level of generality its facts support — broad enough to reach your facts if it truly does, no broader.

## Handling adverse authority — the moves

**Distinguish** (material difference) → **limit** (confine to its holding/context) → **show it's no longer good law** (superseded/abrogated/overruled; confirm via `dingduff-legal-research`'s validity check) → **argue it's unpersuasive** (only against non-binding authority; attack the reasoning, not the court). Confront adverse *binding* authority directly.

## The canons (quick table)

| Canon | Idea | Watch for |
|---|---|---|
| Plain meaning | Clear words control; if clear, stop. | A "plain" reading yielding absurd results invites rebuttal. |
| *Expressio unius* | Listing some excludes the unlisted. | Only if the list is exhaustive, not illustrative. |
| *Ejusdem generis* | Catch-all confined to the list's class. | Identify the class honestly. |
| *Noscitur a sociis* | A word takes color from its neighbors. | For an ambiguous term in a series. |
| Consistent usage | Same term = same meaning; different = different. | Defined terms control over ordinary meaning. |
| Whole-act / structure | Read against headings, definitions, related sections. | The scheme can resolve a local ambiguity. |
| Surplusage | Avoid readings that make words redundant. | — |

Order of use: **text → linguistic canons → structure → extrinsic/substantive.**

## Going further (high-stakes matters)

- **Map every analogy and distinction** for each key case — those that help you (and their strength), and those the **opponent** will draw; prepare the rebuttal.
- **Marshal and rebut every canon** — surface dueling canons (one expands, another narrows), argue why yours governs here, and answer the opposing canon rather than ignoring it.
- **Trace authority chains** — a holding's reach is often set by how later cases read it; argue from the line, not just the point.
- **Neutralize each adverse authority in layers** — primary ground, fallbacks, the opponent's best use of it, and a candor check for binding adverse cases.
