# Rule Synthesis

Method for stating the governing rule the rest of the analysis is built on. Output: for each issue, a precise rule statement, its structure (elements / factors / standard, with the logical relationships), and an authority map showing which source supplies each part.

## The rule is built from retrieved authority, never overstated

Every component traces to a **retrieved** statute, opinion, or regulation — not memory, not a snippet. State the rule at the level of generality the authority actually supports: do not broaden a holding into a sweeping rule or narrow a broad rule to fit your facts. Respect the **weight of authority** (binding > persuasive; higher court > lower; more recent and on-point > older and tangential). If a needed part of the rule has no support in the authority you have, flag it and escalate — don't invent it.

## The four kinds of rules

Knowing which you have tells you what it takes to satisfy or defeat it:

- **Mandatory** — requires conduct/result if triggered ("must," "shall"). Satisfied by showing the trigger is (or isn't) met.
- **Prohibitory** — forbids conduct ("may not," "unlawful"). Turn on whether conduct fell within the prohibition.
- **Discretionary** — permits/empowers ("may," "in the court's discretion"). Show the precondition, then how discretion should be exercised.
- **Declaratory** — defines a status ("means," "constitutes a"). Turn on whether the facts fit the definition.

## Structuring the rule

Decide its shape — this dictates how it is proved and applied:

- **Elements** — conjunctive; *all* required. Analyze element-by-element; any unmet element defeats the claim.
- **Factors** — weighed; none alone dispositive (balancing). Weigh them together.
- **Standard** — a single evaluative measure (reasonableness, good faith, materiality). Argue where the facts fall.
- **Hybrid** — e.g., a threshold element plus balancing; burden-shifting frameworks.

Make connectors explicit: *and* vs. *or*, exceptions, presumptions, who bears the burden on each part.

## Synthesizing across authorities

When no single source states the whole rule, build it inductively: read the cases together, extract the common rule, and where each adds a piece (one defines an element, another adds an exception, a third sets the standard of proof), assemble the pieces into one unified statement and tag each piece to its source. State the synthesized rule once, cleanly, then note the supporting authority — don't make the reader reconstruct it from a case-by-case recitation.

## Reconciling conflicts

1. **Harmonize first** — an apparent conflict often dissolves on the facts (different contexts/postures). Prefer a reading that lets the authorities coexist.
2. **If they truly conflict, rank** — binding over persuasive; higher court over lower; later over earlier within a court; majority over minority.
3. **If it's a genuine split**, say so — state the competing formulations, identify which controls in the operative jurisdiction, and carry the split forward (it becomes an argument, handled in `applying-authority.md`).

## Weight-of-authority hierarchy

Strongest to weakest, in the operative jurisdiction: (1) binding constitutional/statutory text; (2) binding precedent of the controlling court (higher > lower; later > earlier); (3) persuasive precedent — sister jurisdictions, lower courts, dicta — ranked by court level, recency, on-point-ness, reasoning quality; (4) secondary authority (treatises, Restatements) — never controlling, but useful to show a synthesized or majority rule.

## Worked example (illustrative; placeholder cites)

Issue: when does a "no-trespassing" sign defeat the implied license to approach a front door?
- *Case A* (controlling): officers may approach a front door as any visitor would (implied license).
- *Case B* (controlling): a clear, conspicuous "no trespassing" sign at the entry revokes that license.
- *Case C* (persuasive): a sign not visible from the path did **not** revoke it.

Synthesized: *An officer may approach the front door under the implied license extended to ordinary visitors (A), unless the resident revoked it by a notice that a reasonable visitor on the normal route would see and understand (B, C).* Structure: a baseline rule + an exception that is itself a **standard** (clear, conspicuous, visible to a reasonable visitor) — which is what most cases will turn on, and becomes its own proof unit.

## Going further (high-stakes matters)

- **Every formulation** — collect how the rule is phrased across authorities (e.g., "substantial factor" vs. "but-for"); lay them side by side; identify which controls and how the others differ.
- **Definitional architecture** — build the full definition tree for every operative term, following nested definitions to the bottom; flag undefined/contested terms (where the matter will be fought).
- **Map the splits** — chart each camp, its leading authority, and where each jurisdiction stands; locate the operative jurisdiction (or predict it).
- **Trajectory** — trace where the rule came from and where it's heading (expanding/contracting/eroding); pair with `dingduff-legal-research`'s currency checks so you aren't synthesizing from a superseded formulation.
- **Interactions** — map exceptions, affirmative defenses, related/competing rules, and burden/presumption rules, so a technically-correct rule isn't wrong in operation because a defense swallows it.
